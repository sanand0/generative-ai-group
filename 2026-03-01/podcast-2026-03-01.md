Alex: This week’s chat was packed. The big theme, I think, is that AI is moving from “smart model” to “smart system.”
Maya: Yes, and the group kept circling the same idea from different angles: harnesses, tools, memory, and deployment choices now matter as much as the model itself.

Alex: Let’s start with the “model trained for the harness” idea, because that came up a lot.
Maya: Right. Jyotirmay Khebudkar shared that article and said it confirmed, for the first time, why Claude models may have won even though OpenAI was early. His phrase was “your wrapper guides the model.”
Alex: And then Sandeep Srinivasa built on it with a really practical point: tools and instructions in the system prompt during rollouts become part of the model’s distribution. In plain English, if a model is trained and tested in the same kind of environment, it gets much better at real work.
Maya: That’s why the group kept saying the harness is the product. The harness is the surrounding software that lets the model act: tools, loops, retries, memory, and verification.
Alex: Srihari Sriraman connected this to Codex and said the model and harness aren’t separate pieces slapped together later. They’re co-designed.
Maya: The takeaway for normal people is simple: don’t just ask “which model is best?” Ask “which system actually fits my task?” For coding, support, research, and workflows, the wrapper may matter more than the brand name on the model.

Alex: Another big topic was enterprise adoption, especially around GitHub Copilot and Claude Code.
Maya: Jyotirmay Khebudkar said something very relatable: in his healthcare enterprise, GitHub Copilot is the only approved tool. He wondered why there’s so little public discussion about it.
Alex: The answers were interesting. One point was that Microsoft has huge enterprise distribution power, so Copilot feels “official.”
Maya: And another was cost. Jyotirmay noted that Copilot can come bundled through GitHub Enterprise, while getting real value from Claude Code can mean spending around $100 per developer or more.
Alex: Mohamed Yasser also said a $100 Max plan is enough for most of his team, but the API costs are still “ridiculous.”
Maya: The practical insight here is that enterprise AI adoption is not just about quality. It’s about approval, procurement, trust, and how easy it is to buy without creating a security headache.

Alex: There was also a lot of discussion about open versus closed systems, and whether open-source harnesses can win.
Maya: Sandeep Srinivasa argued that open-source harnesses may fail if they don’t have a paired model trained alongside them. Sankalp Shubham agreed and said the harness and model need to be co-designed and co-evolved.
Alex: That led to some sharp comments about traces. Sandeep said Claude Code’s huge usage has given it tons of traces, which creates a very hard-to-beat advantage.
Maya: That matters because traces are real training fuel. If a product learns from millions of real tasks, it can get better in ways that benchmarks don’t capture.
Alex: Ojasvi Yadav mentioned using the A2A protocol for agent-to-agent communication, and Reef in Praval for orchestration and memory.
Maya: For listeners, the non-obvious takeaway is that “open source” is not enough by itself. You need usage data, training loops, and a way to improve from real work.

Alex: Memory came up in a more philosophical way too.
Maya: Jyotirmay Khebudkar shared a post about psychology and AI memory, then asked whether emotions could help encode memory in LLMs.
Alex: And he pushed back on the idea of copying human memory too closely. He said we can reuse some parts and invent the rest. I loved his Wright brothers analogy: they didn’t build a giant flapping bird.
Maya: He also compared submarines and fish, which is a great reminder that good engineering often copies the principle, not the form.
Alex: The practical point is that AI memory should probably be designed for usefulness, not human realism. If a system remembers the right facts, at the right time, that may matter more than whether the memory “feels human.”

Maya: A related thread was about retrieval and research quality. Hari Balaji asked how to stop a deep research agent from trusting bad websites.
Alex: The suggestions were very practical. Paras Chopra said to build a dataset of trusted sources, rerank by trust, restrict by domain like .gov.in where appropriate, and use recency scoring.
Maya: Ankur Pandey suggested NewsGuard and Google Programmable Search Engine. Ojasvi Yadav added that newer sources can be weighted more heavily when sources conflict.
Alex: That’s a good reminder that research agents need source hygiene. If you don’t control source quality, your agent can be factually off even without hallucinating.
Maya: In other words, bad sources can poison an otherwise “accurate” system.

Alex: Another thread that got attention was the economics of frontier AI and the giant money flows around it.
Maya: Paras Chopra discussed OpenAI’s huge funding round and compared it to Microsoft and AWS strategy. People noted that Microsoft may be losing some of its grip while OpenAI expands across consumer and enterprise.
Alex: Aiswarya Sankar brought in Dario Amodei’s prediction that AI could hit trillions in revenue by 2028 or 2029.
Maya: That sounds wild, but the group’s point was that the market is still early. Aiswarya also reminded everyone that most people on Earth still haven’t used AI at all.
Alex: Jyotirmay’s bubble chart post made that even clearer: 84% have never used AI, 16% have used a free chatbot, and a tiny fraction pay for coding tools.
Maya: So yes, the room feels saturated. But the broader world is still just getting started.

Alex: There was some real product news too.
Maya: OpenAI introduced WebSockets in the Responses API, which should help with low-latency, long-running agents that do lots of tool calls.
Alex: Anthropic launched Claude Code Remote Control, so you can kick off a task on your machine and keep an eye on it from your phone.
Maya: People liked the idea, but Abhinav Verma said it was a bit glitchy. Still, it shows where things are going: agents that work across devices, not just in one terminal.
Alex: Then Anthropic acquired Vercept for computer-use capabilities, which sparked a lot of discussion about UI agents versus CLI agents.
Maya: Priyank Agrawal made a great point: CLI is very developer-friendly, but UI agents will matter for everyone else, because humans can interrupt, watch, and help more easily in a visual interface.

Alex: The military and sovereignty discussion was intense too.
Maya: It was sparked by Anthropic’s public comments about the Department of War. People had strong reactions across the spectrum.
Alex: Paras Chopra argued that sovereign frontier models may become necessary, while also warning that power diffuses in dangerous ways.
Maya: Ojasvi Yadav pushed back on some of the doomier framing and argued that AI can improve military capability the way it improves coding or medicine, while still needing guardrails.
Alex: One especially interesting line from the chat was Ojasvi saying, “The same way that AI can be used to improve everything else we deal with... surely it can improve military too.”
Maya: The practical takeaway is that AI governance is no longer abstract. Companies, governments, and labs are actively negotiating where the line should be.

Alex: We also got a few very practical tool mentions worth remembering.
Maya: Baseten published an inference engineering book, and people said it was very relevant.
Alex: Mohamed Yasser shared Langfuse for observability, especially for image data and model monitoring.
Maya: Aiswarya Sankar mentioned Modal offering $30 per month, and also pointed people to free LLM API resources.

Maya: And for researchers, there was Memento, which stores successful and failed trajectories so an agent can learn from experience instead of gradients alone. That’s a lovely idea.

Alex: Before we wrap, let’s give listeners a quick tip from this week.
Maya: My tip is: if you’re building any agent or research workflow, start by scoring your sources. Add trust, domain, and recency into the retrieval step before you blame the model.
Alex: My tip is: stop comparing models only by benchmark scores. Test them inside the real harness you’ll actually use, with your tools, your prompts, and your failure modes.

Maya: Alex, if you were building a production agent this week, how would you apply that?

Maya: And if you were starting from scratch, how would you apply it?
Alex: I’d ask my team, “What do we actually need the agent to do, and what environment will it live in every day?”
