Alex: Hello and welcome to The Generative AI Group Digest for the week of 22 February 2026!

Maya: We're Alex and Maya.

Alex: This week felt like three big stories kept showing up everywhere: agent tools getting more practical, a lot of hand-wringing about AI writing and virality, and a huge wave of India-focused AI momentum.

Maya: Yes, and underneath all of it was one theme: the gap between what looks impressive on the surface and what actually works in the real world.

Alex: Let’s start with the agent wave, because the group had a ton to say about OpenClaw, Claude Code, Manus, and the whole idea of workflow agents.

Maya: Rajesh Parikh shared the fast.ai piece “Breaking the Spell of Vibe Coding,” and that really set the tone. The big idea is that “vibe coding” can feel magical, but it can also hide sloppy thinking.

Alex: Right, and Rajesh also pointed to interpretability work like sparse autoencoders and “navigable manifolds,” which is a fancy way of saying tools that help you look inside a model’s mind and steer it more safely.

Maya: Then the conversation moved from theory to practice. Abhiram Ravikumar kept coming back to OpenClaw as the product that made agents feel real, not just experimental.

Alex: He said the big reason it went viral was not just hype. It solved the last-mile problem by putting AI inside apps people already use, like WhatsApp, Telegram, and Slack.

Maya: That matters a lot. For non-technical people: if you make someone switch to a brand-new app, adoption is hard. If you meet them where they already are, usage jumps.

Alex: Rajesh and others also noted how easy it is to copy the idea now. Kimi Claw, TinyClaw, Go-only clones, lots of variants.

Maya: But the interesting takeaway is that copying the interface is easy. Copying the timing, the model quality, the distribution, and the trust is much harder.

Alex: Sandeep Srinivasa made another strong point: “whoever owns the workflow also owns the application for most large domains.”

Maya: That’s huge. It means the battle is no longer just about chatbots. It’s about who sits inside the process people use to get work done.

Alex: Mehul Nath Jindal added a practical angle with skills and frameworks. He asked whether Claude Agent SDK can work with non-Claude models, and the answer was yes, in some cases, but quality and cost can change a lot.

Maya: Varun Jain said something really useful there: when they compared a workflow agent to Claude Agent SDK with the same tools and structure, the SDK version was at par or slightly better, but 5 to 10 times more expensive and slower.

Alex: So the lesson is not “always use the fanciest agent.” It’s “use the right amount of search for the job.”

Maya: Exactly. And Ojasvi Yadav gave a simple rule of thumb: if it’s a conversation with state updates, use Claude Agent SDK. If it’s a structured workflow, use LangGraph. If it’s deep research, use DeepAgents.

Alex: That kind of guidance is gold because it turns all this buzz into decisions.

Maya: Another practical thread was local and private AI. Rajesh asked about local AI PCs for in-campus privacy use cases, and people pointed to options like JOHNAIC and full-size boxes instead of just SBCs.

Alex: That’s a real business need. Not every team can send sensitive work to the cloud.

Maya: And that leads neatly into another recurring theme: model and harness quality are increasingly blending together.

Alex: Vaibhav Bhargava said the lines between the harness and the model are blurring, especially with Anthropic and OpenAI post-training models to work better in their own agent environments.

Maya: “Harness” here means the surrounding software that tells the model what tools it has, how to think, and how to act. It’s not just the model anymore; it’s the whole system around it.

Alex: Which is why Vaibhav’s workaround was clever: use `ANTHROPIC_DEFAULT_HAIKU_MODEL` to silently spin up Sonnet 4.6 with a 1M context window underneath.

Maya: Sneaky, but very practical. It shows people are optimizing for what actually works, not just what the label says.

Alex: Rajesh also shared a note on test-time scaling as “trial and error,” basically internalizing pass@k.

Maya: That’s a useful phrase. Pass@k means “if you try k times, how likely are you to get one good result.” Test-time scaling is just letting the model think, search, try, and refine more before answering.

Alex: Ashish and Rajesh then compared this with science, where you have design of experiments and response surface modeling.

Maya: And Rajesh’s point was key: in chemistry, medicine, and mechanics, you can’t just let GPUs go brrr. Physical experiments cost money and time, so you need smarter search methods.

Alex: So a big non-obvious takeaway is that agent design is becoming a search problem under constraints, not just a prompt problem.

Maya: Exactly. Let’s jump to the second big theme: AI writing, slop, and virality.

Alex: Oh yes. This was everywhere. Mahesh Chandran G had some of the sharpest takes.

Maya: He argued that a lot of AI writing is bad not because it’s “too high entropy” or too random, but because it adds rhetorical flourishes without real substance.

Alex: That’s a very human complaint. The words sound polished, but the message is empty.

Maya: He also said something important: people complain about AI slop, but their revealed preferences tell a different story. They still engage with it.

Alex: That’s a brutal but fair point. If people click, share, and read it, the market is rewarding it whether they admit it or not.

Maya: He even said the average online user may not care whether content is AI or human, as long as it’s entertaining enough.

Alex: Atharva Raykar picked up on that and joked that our true master is now ChatGPT.

Maya: Funny, but it lands because so much of the internet now sits in a loop of outputs designed for engagement. Mahesh’s line about humanity becoming reward models for AI is striking.

Alex: Meaning we’re basically training systems with likes, comments, and attention signals.

Maya: Yes. And that’s where virality comes in. Abhiram Ravikumar spent a lot of time dissecting OpenClaw’s spread.

Alex: He said virality comes from volume, timing, product-market fit, and randomness.

Maya: The most useful line from him was: “My meta point is that all this dissection doesn’t help with virality.”

Alex: That’s true and uncomfortable. We love post-hoc explanations, but most viral hits are still partly luck.

Maya: Still, he did identify a few repeatable things: ship many hooks, let usage happen in public spaces, and make the product itself the content.

Alex: Like showing activity in a live timeline or using a meme people want to share.

Maya: Right. That’s practical. If you’re building a consumer product, don’t just think about functionality. Think about what becomes visible to others.

Alex: There was also a whole side thread on AI writing style. Ayush asked how to copy writing style from thousands of samples, and the group discussed prompt examples, semantic retrieval, and supervised fine-tuning.

Maya: Ojasvi said if you have a few categories, use representative in-context examples; if not, use similarity search to pull the right examples in. If you want to go further, use DSPy bootstrap few-shot.

Alex: And Abhinav said SFT should work, which is supervised fine-tuning, meaning training the model on your examples.

Maya: The practical takeaway is simple: don’t jump straight to training if better prompting or retrieval can solve it.

Alex: We should also mention the social side of AI writing. Mahesh said he used to strongly dislike people passing AI output off as their own, but now nearly everyone does it.

Maya: That’s a cultural shift worth noticing. People may still use AI for critique, structure, or polishing, but the line around ownership is getting fuzzier.

Alex: Which creates a new trust problem. If everyone sounds polished, how do you know who actually has experience?

Maya: The answer may be that experience still leaks through. Deep domain knowledge is hard to fake for long.

Alex: That brings us to the third big theme: India’s AI ecosystem, sovereign models, and the hardware story.

Maya: This was huge. Sarvam came up again and again. People were clearly excited.

Alex: Mehul Nath Jindal said Sarvam’s 30B and 105B open-source models seriously compete with Qwen3 in their size class.

Maya: That’s a big deal for Indian AI. It means a local team is building models that people actually respect on performance, not just on symbolism.

Alex: There was also excitement around Sarvam building Meta Ray-Ban-style glasses, and people called it “Sovereign OpenAI + Meta + Anthropic” in one conversation.

Maya: And the phrase “Sarvam is the main character” definitely captured the mood.

Alex: The other major India-specific thread was OpenAI for India and the idea that big labs are now paying close attention to the market.

Maya: Mehul also said “stargate India = OpenAI x Tata,” which gives you a sense of how seriously people are reading the infrastructure angle.

Alex: Infrastructure matters because a lot of these products need compute, local deployment, and maybe offline capability.

Maya: Exactly. Mehul shared a case about Eka Care building an offline-capable medical scribe with NVIDIA AI. That matters in healthcare, where connectivity, privacy, and specialized terminology all matter.

Alex: And he pointed out something smart: in medicine, transcription is only part of the value. The bigger business problem is coding and billing correctly.

Maya: That’s a great example of how AI often wins by helping with the economically important step, not the obviously flashy one.

Alex: Harshit Agarwal also shared news about India’s major AI supercomputer deployment in New Delhi, built with G42, MBZUAI, and Cerebras.

Maya: The phrase “sovereign AI” came up a lot. In plain English, it means a country wants its own compute, data, and control rather than depending entirely on foreign infrastructure.

Alex: Which is why people were so interested in local deployment, local AI PCs, and offline-first products.

Maya: There was even a discussion on whether India is missing the application-layer wave. Sandeep warned that whoever owns the workflow owns the app, so just hoping to remain “the app layer” may be a weak strategy.

Alex: That’s an important warning. If workflows move under the model, the surface area for standalone apps shrinks.

Maya: On the flip side, there’s a lot of optimism that India can build in healthcare, enterprise, and public infrastructure where local context matters more than generic global virality.

Alex: And the group definitely thinks the timing is right.

Maya: Before we wrap, we should touch on one more practical theme: benchmarking and overfitting.

Alex: Yes. Rajesh shared François Chollet’s comment that frontier models are overfitting to ARC’s original encoding format.

Maya: That means models can look strong on a benchmark without really being generally smart. They may just be getting good at that exact test shape.

Alex: That ties back to a lot of the other conversations this week. Whether it’s benchmarks, writing style, or virality, surface performance can be misleading.

Maya: Right. The deeper question is always: does it work in real life, in messy conditions, with real users?

Alex: So here are the quick actionable tips from this week.

Maya: My tip is: when building an agent, start with the simplest framework that matches the task. Conversation with memory? Use an agent SDK. Structured workflow? Use LangGraph. Deep research? Use DeepAgents.

Alex: And my tip is: if you’re building a product, ask what part of the workflow can become visible or shareable. That’s often where adoption and virality come from, not from marketing alone.

Maya: Alex, how would you apply that if you were shipping a new AI tool this month?

Alex: I’d design the workflow first, then decide whether the AI sits in chat, in-app actions, or public artifacts like logs or timelines. I’d avoid adding complexity just because a model can do it.

Maya: Perfect. And I’d add: don’t trust polish alone. Ask where the substance is, and whether the system works outside the demo.
