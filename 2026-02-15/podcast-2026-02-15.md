Maya: Exactly. And that’s where context engineering starts to matter as much as prompting.

Alex: A lot of people were also comparing models for real tasks, especially OCR. Mohamed Yasser asked for the best model to parse a 10,000-question scanned textbook.
Maya: This turned into a really practical discussion. People suggested Sarvam, Mistral OCR, DeepSeek OCR, and GLM OCR.
Alex: The key insight was that if the scans are decent and mostly English, many models will work.
Maya: But for mixed English and Indic text, Sarvam got a lot of love. Mohamed Yasser said he tested Sarvam on Hindi book images and found it pretty accurate.
Alex: He also noted that GLM OCR has “unreal performance” and that the benchmarks are looking very strong.
Maya: The non-obvious takeaway is that “OCR” isn’t one thing anymore. It can be a tiny specialist model, or a larger vision model, depending on cost, speed, language, and accuracy needs.
Alex: And Mohamed kept repeating a useful idea: sometimes the lazy 99% solution is the right one.
Maya: That’s good engineering advice. Don’t overbuild if a good-enough OCR pass solves the job.

Alex: Voice agents had their own big section too. PIYUSH MAKHIJA asked about closing the accuracy gap between WebRTC and phone audio.
Maya: Mohamed Yasser gave a very useful answer. He asked whether the team was using the right audio format, like ulaw, PCM, or alaw, because Twilio and phone pipelines often behave differently.
Alex: So the lesson is simple: bad voice results are often not just the model.
Maya: Right. Format, sample rate, and training data matter. He also noted that Sarvam trained ASR specifically for those phone-like scenarios, and that hotwords can help for a small set of domain terms.
Alex: And for multilingual systems, he suggested translating to English before passing text to the LLM in some cases.
Maya: That’s a very practical rule of thumb. If your agent stack is struggling, check the audio plumbing before blaming the model.

Alex: There was also a lot of talk about agent frameworks and “always-on” systems. People shared OpenClaw, LocalGPT, Rowboat, Nanobot, Picoclaw, and message-flow.
Maya: That’s a sign the community is moving from chat demos to real agent infrastructure. Persistent memory, local deployment, tool use, and observability are becoming the real product.
Alex: One person asked about running OpenClaw with AirLLM on low VRAM, which shows how quickly people are trying to push these systems onto local hardware.
Maya: And someone else asked how to use Claude Code with Blender or Rive MCPs. So it’s not just text anymore. It’s design tools, media tools, and workflows that touch the whole stack.

Alex: Another big topic was the “software factory” idea and how far autonomous coding can really go.
Maya: Moghal linked to Simon Willison’s piece, and the thread around it kept coming back to the same tension: AI can build fast, but long-term maintainability is still hard.
Alex: That’s why the best teams seem to use AI for generation, then human judgment for architecture, testing, and cleanup.
Maya: It also explains why several people are talking about agents being better at code review than at first-pass creation.
