Maya: That’s such a good line. In plain English, the point is that the hard, messy part of making things is still the important part. AI can speed up the first draft, but it can’t replace the learning that comes from struggling through the work.

Alex: Right, and Ashwin even asked if this is the bitter lesson of creativity. That’s a smart question. The “bitter lesson” usually means scale and brute force often win in AI, but here the twist is that creativity may still need friction. You can’t shortcut taste, judgment, or craft forever.

Maya: That matters for writers, designers, coders, everyone really. If you use AI only to avoid effort, you may get average output faster. If you use it to get through the boring parts while still doing the thinking yourself, you probably grow faster.

Alex: Ashwin also shared a small video on the Kardashev scale, which is the idea of measuring civilizations by how much energy they can use.

Maya: That’s one of those “fun but useful” ideas because it pushes you to think beyond today’s model releases. It’s a reminder that AI conversations are also really conversations about infrastructure, energy, and what scale looks like for the next decade.

Alex: And tied to that, Ashwin said the battery tech and K1 sci-fi conversation could move to Watercooler/Banter in #random. Classic group behavior.

Maya: Very on brand. Okay, second big theme: agents are becoming less about chatbots and more about coordination. Ashwin highlighted Google’s fresh “2026 Agentic Outlook,” and said the enterprise story is shifting from “chatbots” to “A2A,” which means agent-to-agent communication.

Alex: That’s a huge shift. The idea is that one agent might talk directly to another, like a Salesforce agent working with a Google Cloud agent, without a human clicking every step.

Maya: Ashwin’s line was that “human-in-the-loop” is moving to “human-on-the-loop.” In other words, people supervise and intervene when needed, but they’re not sitting there doing every handoff.

Alex: That matters because it tells us where enterprise AI is headed. The model itself is becoming less important than the system around it: protocols, interoperability, and how tools and agents cooperate.

Maya: And that connects to another theme from Sandeep Srinivasa and others about orchestration. Sandeep shared NVIDIA’s ToolOrchestra and Cursor’s agent harness blog, and the core idea was simple: intelligence can come from coordinating smaller tools and sub-agents, not just one giant model trying to do everything.

Alex: In non-technical terms, orchestration is just “getting the right helper to do the right job at the right time.”

Maya: Exactly. That’s why people kept asking about Claude Code, Cursor, skills, MCP, and agent frameworks. The ecosystem is moving from “Which model is smartest?” to “Which setup gets work done best?”

Alex: That showed up in the long Claude Code versus Cursor thread too. Sandeep said Claude Code can be more codebase-aware and really shines for devops and command-line work, while Cursor is still strong, especially if you tune rules and MCPs well.

Maya: And Karthik Sashidhar made an important point: Claude Code is less intrusive. It helps when asked. Cursor feels more like autocomplete and can get annoying when it’s wrong.

Alex: That’s a practical takeaway for listeners. If your work is a lot of terminal, infra, and agentic workflows, Claude Code may feel better. If you want a more integrated editor experience, Cursor may still be the smoother daily driver.

Maya: Sandeep also mentioned using “skills,” which are basically little packaged instructions or workflows that guide the agent. He even pointed to github.com/obra/superpowers and said it automatically brainstorms, writes tests, executes, and code reviews with less intervention.

Alex: The non-obvious takeaway here is that skills are becoming a form of reusable process memory. They’re not just prompts; they’re a way to encode how your team works.

Maya: That ties nicely into Pulkit Gupta’s question about skills for non-technical users. Jyotirmay Khebudkar shared a very practical example: he uses a Gamma skill for decks and a morning briefing skill that scans email, calendar, WhatsApp, and Slack to find blockers.

Alex: That’s the kind of thing that shows skills are not just for coding. They can become personal workflows. But Jyotirmay also made the strong point that sharing skills is one thing, but scaling them in a team is another. Markdown files alone may not be enough.

Maya: And he’s right. Once a skill becomes code plus markdown plus workflow rules, you need better packaging, versioning, and marketplaces. That’s why people were asking whether Github, npm, or some new skill marketplace will become the home for them.

Alex: Another big cluster of discussion was memory and personalization. Thine’s Siddhartha joined in through Nishanth’s thread and said they’re working on long-term memory and personal context with an iOS app that can record 24/7 so nothing important slips through.

Maya: That’s wild, but it makes sense. A lot of the current AI products are still stateless or shallowly stateful. Real value appears when the system remembers your habits, your goals, and your style over time.

Alex: Ashish added a useful idea too: he runs a meta Claude agent at the end of a session that updates global and project-specific claude.md files and skills from the conversation. That’s a very practical form of continual learning without changing the model itself.

Maya: And that’s the key distinction. Most of the “learning” people want from agents today can happen outside the weights. You can improve the system by saving better context, better instructions, and better workflows.

Alex: On the research side, Abhiram Ravikumar shared an NLP paper alert about End-to-End Test-Time Training for Long Context. The simple version is that the model keeps learning from the context it sees at inference time.

Maya: In normal language: the model doesn’t just read the prompt and answer. It also updates itself a bit while answering, using the same next-token prediction objective it used in training.

Alex: That matters because long-context use cases are one of the biggest pain points in AI. If the model can compress useful information into its weights during use, it could handle much longer tasks better.

Maya: Abhiram also asked about data storage for a multi-agent B2B chatbot, and Sandeep gave the most grounded answer of the week: for 3,000 sessions a day, just use Postgres or MySQL.

Alex: That’s a great reminder. A lot of teams overcomplicate infrastructure too early. The real challenge is often not raw scale, but how to query, analyze, and support the product experience around the conversations.

Maya: Sandeep also suggested ClickHouse or S3/Blob plus Athena for analytics, depending on whether the data is hot or just for learning and reporting. So the practical split is transactional storage versus analytical storage.

Alex: We also saw a strong thread on traces and evals. Sukuru Sai Vineet asked about storing AI SDK traces for continuous learning, and Sandeep said MotherDuck, S3, ClickHouse, and Athena can all work depending on what you need.

Maya: The deeper lesson there is that evals are still messy. Sukuru said Judgment Labs felt mediocre because every org ends up building custom eval setups anyway.

Alex: That lines up with the broader vibe of the group: yes, tools are improving, but teams still want control over their own feedback loops.

Maya: Another fun thread was about model behavior and time. Sandeep shared that Gemini seems uncomfortable with the idea that it might be 2026, and that in its thinking traces it refers to things like “the user’s time” as if it’s separate from its own.

Alex: That’s fascinating because it points to something people don’t think about enough: models don’t “experience” time like we do. They operate in token space, but training and prompting can make them sound like they do.

Maya: Sandeep also responded to a question about why models get sarcastic, and he made the point that there’s no such thing as “natural” behavior after RLHF, which is reinforcement learning from human feedback.

Alex: In plain words, the humor and persona are shaped by preference data. So if a model jokes, that’s not personality in a human sense. It’s a learned style that people seemed to like.

Maya: We should touch on vertical AI too. Abhinav Verma shared ChatGPT Health and noted that ChatGPT gets health questions from 40 million people a day. That’s a huge signal.

Alex: And then the discussion moved to OpenEvidence and doctor-focused tools. Pulkit Gupta made a strong point that OpenEvidence is different because it serves medical professionals with peer-reviewed citations and a real data moat.

Maya: Jyotirmay added a good nuance: in some regulated areas, like medicine, AI may first win on narrow tasks like refill prescriptions or chronic care support, not full autonomous diagnosis.

Alex: That’s important. Vertical AI doesn’t just mean “AI for doctors” or “AI for lawyers.” It means AI that fits the workflow, the regulation, the trust model, and the data source.

Maya: We also had a practical question about Arabic model quality. Sandeep asked which model works best for Arabic text generation, and Abhiram suggested Qwen, while Sandeep also mentioned Gemini 3, Gemini 2.5 Pro, and Falcon H1 Arabic.

Alex: That’s a reminder that model choice is often language- and task-specific. What works well in English may not be best for Arabic, and teams need to test on their real use case.

Maya: One more thread I really liked was the discussion around voice. Nishanth shared consumer AI predictions saying voice is a great extra interface but a bad primary interface.

Alex: That feels right. Voice is convenient in some settings, but a lot of tasks need visual feedback, editing, and quiet environments. Text still wins for precision and privacy.

Maya: He also said, and I think this is the key insight, that the brain processes vision differently from voice, which makes visual interfaces more addictive and useful for many tasks.

Alex: Before we wrap, let’s hit one listener tip each.

Maya: Mine is this: if you’re building an AI workflow, store the process, not just the prompt. Save your best instructions, rules, and examples in something reusable like Claude skills, claude.md, or a simple workflow doc. Alex, how would you apply that in a real project?

Alex: I’d say start small: pick one repetitive task, like meeting summaries or code review prep, and turn the steps into a skill or checklist. Then test whether Claude Code, Cursor, or the Claude Agent SDK does it better. Maya, how would you choose between them?

Maya: I’d choose based on the work. If it’s terminal and infra, Claude Code. If it’s editor-first coding, Cursor. If it’s a custom product, then Claude Agent SDK, CopilotKit, or the new protocols like A2UI and AGUI. Alex, what’s your tip?

Alex: Mine is don’t overbuild storage too early. If you’re at a few thousand sessions a day, Postgres is probably enough, and you can add ClickHouse or Athena later for analytics. Maya, how would you keep that simple in practice?

Maya: I’d separate “what the app needs to run” from “what the team wants to learn.” Put operational data in Postgres or MySQL, keep traces and logs in S3 or MotherDuck, and only reach for heavier systems when you actually feel the pain.
