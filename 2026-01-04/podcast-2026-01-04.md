Maya: Next up — observability for multi-agent systems. Sheetal Chauhan asked for frameworks that give full session traces: MCP calls, execution timing, turnaround time.
Alex: People suggested all sorts of tools: Langfuse and Logfire for traces, Arize Phoenix and promptfoo for model evaluation, and the evergreen suggestion — OpenTelemetry, or otel, which is basically a standard way to collect logs, traces and metrics.
Maya: Meenu and Rachitt noted that otel gives you vendor freedom — you can plug it into Jaeger for traces, Prometheus plus Grafana for metrics, or use Logfire if you want a simpler, more integrated approach.
Alex: Why this matters: multi-agent apps spread behavior across many services and model calls. Without standard tracing you can't answer basic questions like "Which sub-agent slowed down?" or "Where did context get lost?"
Maya: Non-obvious takeaway: otel is worth the upfront complexity because it lets you mix-and-match backends later. If you expect to run Claude skills, OpenAI SDKs, or custom agents, instrumenting at the otel layer future-proofs your telemetry.
Alex: Practical idea: start with minimal tracing around session IDs and critical RPCs, ship that to a simple backend like Jaeger or Logfire, and iteratively add metrics for latency and error types.

Maya: Third topic — domain-specific evaluation and human-in-the-loop design. Ashwin asked about domain evals and Bharat recommended hiring domain experts and combining human judges with LLM-based judge evals.

Alex: Why it matters: general benchmarks miss edge cases that matter in specific industries. A model that "scores well" can still fail catastrophically on the 10% of cases where domain knowledge matters.
Maya: Practical idea: build small human-eval loops for high-risk slices early. Use LLM judging to triage results, but keep humans in the loop for final judgments and rubric updates.
