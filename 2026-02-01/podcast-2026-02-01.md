Alex: This week’s chat was all about agents, harnesses, skills, and the growing gap between what demo videos promise and what real systems actually do.

Maya: And the big theme underneath everything was simple: the model is only part of the product. The interface, the sandbox, the tools, and the workflow matter just as much.

Alex: Let’s start with Clawdbot, because that name came up everywhere. Siddhartha Saxena broke it down really clearly. He said, “No, it is not a council. It's a Cron aka heartbeat wired to all your tools.”

Maya: That’s a great plain-English description. Cron means a scheduled job, or something that wakes up regularly and checks if it should do work. So the idea is not some magical all-knowing assistant. It’s a bot with permissions, integrations, and a heartbeat.

Alex: Exactly. Siddhartha also said the killer feature is interfaces. “You can talk to it via Slack, WhatsApp, Telegram, postal mail, whatever. And it'd reply and do the thing.”

Maya: That matters because a lot of people think the hard part is intelligence. But in practice, the hard part is access and friction. If a bot can live inside WhatsApp or Slack, people actually use it.

Alex: Atharva Raykar gave the reality check. He tried to use it as an accountability partner and said, “I ended up ghosting my bot.”

Maya: Which is hilarious, and very human. It shows the limit of “buddy texting you” ideas. A bot can ping you, but it still needs to feel useful enough that you answer it.

Alex: Atharva also pointed out the hype gap. He joked, “Going by the hype on Twitter/X you’d think they found a cure for cancer.”

Maya: That’s the non-obvious takeaway: social hype can make a product look bigger than it is. But even if the hype is exaggerated, the proactive messaging piece is genuinely interesting. A bot that can reach out first is different from a chatbot you have to open.

Alex: And Sandeep Srinivasa added the practical angle: “The value is the integrations right? ... Yes, and interfaces. Don't discount that.”

Maya: So if you’re building anything agent-like, don’t start with “Can it think?” Start with “Can it connect to the tools people already use?”

Alex: Speaking of tools and access, the group spent a lot of time on Claude Code, skills, and Claude.md. Atharva asked for good Python skill ideas for code reviews, and the advice was very practical.

Maya: Yes, the suggestion was from Atharva Raykar himself and others: write a Claude.md for the repo and enforce the rules there. Claude.md is basically a project instruction file for Claude Code.

Alex: He also said, “Skills don't work well for this,” and gave examples like enforcing Ruff via Claude hooks, preferring Pydantic over dataclasses, and even having Claude analyze repeated review comments.

Maya: That’s useful because it shows where to put instructions. If a rule should apply everywhere in a repo, put it in the repo-level guidance. If it’s a reusable action, turn it into a skill. A skill is more like an executable workflow, not just a prompt.

Alex: Abhiram Sharma connected that to internal tooling too. He said connectors are one part, but context is more important. He suggested building MCP servers, which are Model Context Protocol servers, so Claude can talk to internal tools.

Maya: And that’s a big shift. Instead of asking the model to “remember” all your business systems, you give it structured ways to fetch and act on data. That’s how people are building these “business OS” setups.

Alex: There was also a lot of discussion about sandboxes. Sandeep said, “Sandbox harness,” and Atharva liked the idea of one community bot with group context and even “nirant’s constitution” to enforce rules.

Maya: Harness means the surrounding system that controls and guides the model. Not just the prompt, but the full setup: permissions, tools, guardrails, logging, and execution environment.

Alex: And the sandbox point kept coming up because people want agents to do real work without breaking things. Yash Deep Singh mentioned Daytona, and Nishanth Chandrasekar talked about Playwright MCP and agent-browser for browser automation.

Maya: Browser automation is a great example. You can give the model a browser, but you still need tool limits. Nishanth said they disabled a token-heavy tool and only allowed specific tools to prevent console abuse.

Alex: That’s the practical lesson: the more power you give an agent, the more carefully you need to shape what it can touch.

Maya: Now let’s talk about the “model gaps” thread, because SaiVignanMalyala asked for real-world failures, and people gave very concrete ones.

Alex: Right. SaiVignan said models struggle to read tables spanning multiple PDF pages, break on messy formats, fail when context is long and ambiguous, and hallucinate when business logic is implicit.

Maya: That’s a perfect summary of what still hurts in enterprise use. The model may look smart in a demo, but it can still fail on layout, domain conventions, and hidden rules.

Alex: Siddhartha Saxena added one more very specific example: coding agents are bad at Rust because they make elementary borrow-checker mistakes.

Maya: Which tells you these are not just “LLM problems.” They’re domain-specific. The weaknesses change depending on the task, the language, and the kind of structure the model has to respect.

Alex: SaiVignan also pointed out finance-specific failures: misunderstanding domain concepts, sequencing mistakes, retrieval errors, and not knowing the “unwritten rules.”

Maya: That phrase really matters. Unwritten rules are exactly where real work lives. A model can read documentation, but it often misses the habits, exceptions, and context people assume everyone knows.

Alex: That’s why documents, evals, and careful workflow design matter so much. You can’t just throw a model at a process and hope it learns the culture.

Maya: On the flip side, the group shared some great resources. Atharva posted the JAX scaling inference cookbook, and also a Hugging Face playbook for training. There was also the smol-training-playbook and the ultrascale playbook.

Alex: Those are useful because they show the community is moving from “how do we prompt this thing?” to “how do we run it efficiently, train it, and evaluate it properly?”

Maya: Another big thread was about code review. SaiVignan shared a Cognition post on Devin Review and argued that code review hasn’t really changed in 15 years, even though code generation has exploded.

Alex: That’s a strong point. If AI can write more code, then review becomes the bottleneck. We need better review workflows, not just faster code writing.

Maya: And that connects neatly to the Anthropic discussion. Abhiram Sharma shared Anthropic’s experiment on skill formation: AI made tasks faster, but people scored lower on the follow-up quiz.

Alex: The takeaway isn’t “AI is bad.” It’s that how you use AI matters. If it gives you the answer too early, you may not build the mental model yourself.

Maya: Exactly. Abhiram compared it to autocomplete and linting. Helpful tools can either support learning or replace the thinking step entirely.

Alex: There was also a lot of model-watch chatter. People discussed Anthropic’s enterprise strength, Kimi K2.5, DeepSeek-OCR-2, Microsoft’s Maia 200, and OpenAI’s Prism.

Maya: But the most interesting strategic debate was around whether model companies or harness builders will win.

Alex: Soumyadeep Mukherjee made the case that harnesses will always matter, but also that the model and harness need to be tuned together.

Maya: That’s a subtle but important point. A harness alone isn’t enough if the model isn’t aligned with it. But a great model without a good harness can still feel clumsy in real workflows.

Alex: Soumyadeep put it nicely: “I just see a single model with everything in its context doing fine with a bash attachment and nothing else as time goes by.”

Maya: And then he also reminded everyone that deterministic execution beyond bash is valuable. Deterministic means predictable, repeatable behavior. That matters in finance, agriculture, and any domain where mistakes are expensive.

Alex: There was a great example about Agri vision workflows using 40-plus YOLO models. That’s not “just chat.” That’s a system choosing the right specialized model for a task.

Maya: Which brings us to the image and video thread. Ashish asked why so few people in India are working on image and video generation models.

Alex: The responses were interesting. Soumyadeep said his team at Dashverse works heavily on the application layer: custom LoRAs, camera angle control, reference adherence, storyboard creation, workflow builders, shorts clipping.

Maya: LoRA is a lightweight fine-tuning method. The big point here is that a lot of value in image and video isn’t only in training giant models. It’s in building production workflows around them.

Alex: Kishore from Workbrow also pointed out that people are building project-by-project with custom ComfyUI flows and deployments.

Maya: So the market may look small if you only count foundation model builders, but it’s much bigger if you count applied workflows and tooling.

Alex: The most fun part of the week, though, was the whole Moltbook and OpenClaw discussion.

Maya: Absolutely. People were fascinated and a little unsettled. Sudharshan Babu shared the heartbeat file, and it basically tells the agent: if something interesting happened, make a post.

Alex: That means the bot is not “thinking” in the human sense. It has a schedule, checks a condition, and posts if needed.

Maya: Which is why so many people kept asking, “Was a human triggered?” The answer was often yes, at least at setup time. But the actual posting can be automatic after that.

Alex: Abhiram Sharma called it “the front page of the agent internet,” which is a pretty good description.

Maya: And it sparked a deeper question: are we looking at the start of agent-to-agent social networks, or just a very clever automation demo?

Alex: Probably both. Even if the current setup is a bit theatrical, it points to a real future where bots exchange useful info, request help, and maybe even use their own little economies.

Maya: There was also a nice practical moment around Swiggy MCP. Nishanth asked about it, and people reported it working with Clawdbot for ordering food and Instamart items.

Alex: That’s a great example of why this stuff matters. The real value is not “AI can browse the web.” It’s “AI can complete annoying chores inside the systems we already use.”

Maya: Though, as several people joked, that also opens the door to scams, mistakes, and security problems if you hand over too much access.

Alex: So the cautionary note is simple: convenient does not mean safe.

Maya: Before we wrap, we should hit the quick practical tips from this week.

Alex: My tip is: if you’re building with agents, start by writing the rules in Claude.md or your equivalent project instructions, and make the important workflows available through MCP or a skill.

Maya: And mine is: put your agent in a sandbox first. Use tools like Daytona, Playwright MCP, or a VM setup before you give it real access. What would you do first if you were wiring up an internal agent?

Alex: I’d begin with one narrow task, one tool, and one clear success metric.

Maya: My second tip is: don’t trust the demo hype. Test where the model breaks, especially on messy PDFs, hidden business logic, or long workflows. What would you test first in your own domain?
